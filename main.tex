\section*{Problem Description}
% ===================================================================

New England SteamWorks (NES) dispatches multiple technicians with service vehicles to perform maintenance jobs across a wide geographic region every week.
The Python scheduling engine is a \textbf{draft-generation and review-support tool}: once each week it takes the eligible candidate jobs, applies written business rules and weekly exceptions, and produces a draft weekly transcription calendar that the owner (Ryan) can review and manually enter into ServiceM8.

The engine is \emph{not} a customer-booking tool, not a ServiceM8 calendar-writing tool, and not the owner of the broader Airtable\,/\,ServiceM8 invite lifecycle.
It should not auto-write the ServiceM8 calendar, directly book customers, or take over downstream invitation management.

\paragraph{Scope guardrail.}
If the solution starts to feel larger than ``produce a reliable weekly draft calendar plus the review artefacts needed to support Ryan's decision,'' it is drifting beyond the intended build.
The engine stays centred on weekly draft schedule generation, review support, and three specifically defined callable workflows (Weekly Run, Replacement Job, Replacement Route).

The weekly planning task proceeds in two deterministic phases:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Phase~1: Weekly Scheduling.} Apply queue-based ranking, score each candidate job, then assign jobs to (technician, vehicle, day) route slots by walking a priority--sorted list and checking eligibility and capacity rules.
    \item \textbf{Phase~2: Daily Stop Ordering.} For each day's assigned job set, arrange stops in a sensible geographic sequence using a nearest-neighbour heuristic.
\end{enumerate}

The engine must:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Serve as many high--priority jobs as possible each week, respecting all business rules;
    \item Produce compact, drivable daily routes;
    \item Make every decision explainable---traceable to a named rule and its inputs;
    \item Produce reviewable route/day proposals and a transcription calendar (not direct calendar writes);
    \item Distinguish assigned jobs, standby jobs, and anchor-hold jobs in its outputs.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{What success looks like.}
Ryan reviews the proposed week, sees a draft calendar that makes practical sense, and can manually transcribe it into ServiceM8 without routine rebuilding.

% ===================================================================
\section*{Key Objectives (KPIs)}
% ===================================================================
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Primary goal:} Serve as many high--priority jobs as possible each week.  Priority is determined by queue placement, aging pressure, geographic quality, and seasonal ranking rules.
    \item \textbf{Secondary goals:} Keep daily workloads roughly even across technicians; keep routes geographically compact; maintain area readiness for customer communication.
    \item \textbf{Acceptance standard:} Most live weeks should be approved by Ryan with no changes.  One imperfect route does not fail the entire week, but if Ryan is routinely rebuilding, the system has not met the goal.
    \item \textbf{Non--goal for Initial Build:} Mathematical optimality.  The engine should produce \emph{good, explainable} plans---not provably optimal ones.
\end{itemize}

\section*{System Overview}
% ===================================================================

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{ServiceM8} -- system of record for jobs, technicians, and operational data.
    \item \textbf{Airtable} -- scheduling rules, policy settings, review/communication layers, and the radiator-hours table.
    \item \textbf{Python Engine} -- rule evaluation, scheduling, stop ordering, output generation, and the three callable workflows (Weekly Run, Replacement Job, Replacement Route).
    \item \textbf{Make\,/\,Zapier} -- orchestration and automation between the above.
\end{itemize}

The Python engine consumes four structured input payloads---\textbf{Candidate Jobs}, \textbf{Weekly Context}, \textbf{Weekly Exceptions}, and \textbf{Lookup\,/\,Rules Data}---and produces structured outputs including route groupings, standby lists, pre-route maps, a transcription calendar, and record-based communications.
The exact transport format is flexible (CSV, JSON, or another structured payload); the engine builds to the required data contract, not one rigid file-name convention.

Every decision the engine makes can be traced to a named rule and the data that triggered it.

\section*{Queue and State Rules}
\label{sec:queues}
% ===================================================================

Queue placement is the main control layer for Initial Build.
The engine consumes queue meaning; it does not invent new queue meaning.
A job cannot be in two queues at once.

\medskip
{\small
\begin{longtable}{lcccp{5.5cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{Queue / State} & \textbf{Eligible?} & \textbf{Relative Treatment} & \textbf{Geo Flex} & \textbf{Notes} \\
\midrule
Urgent          & No  & Manual only             & ---          & Always excluded from weekly engine.  Includes Travel Badge / expedited / emergency / weekend service once bumped to Urgent. \\
On Hold         & No  & Manual only             & ---          & Always excluded.  No special override. \\
Priority        & Yes & Highest manual boost    & Substantial  & Ryan-only assignment by queue placement.  No separate priority-reason field required.  Can justify substantial geography weakening; may serve as anchor. \\
Accepted Quotes & Yes & Below Priority, above RS & Substantial  & Always eligible once accepted.  Treat as always Two-Man in Initial Build.  Substantial geography weakening allowed; fragile route may use anchor-hold. \\
Requested Scheduling & Yes & Fixed boost below Priority & Moderate & Ryan-only catchall boost queue; includes overbooked jobs and other jobs Ryan wants boosted.  Moderate geography weakening allowed.  May serve as anchor. \\
Scheduling Preferences & Yes & Early-pass review pool (not a score boost) & Moderate if needed & Clear preferences are hard rules; vague notes \textbf{must be flagged for Ryan and not scheduled until clarified}. \end{longtable}
}

\paragraph{Rebook rule.}
If a customer rebooks and leaves scheduling notes or preferences, the job keeps its place in line.
If the customer rebooks with no notes, the job loses its place and aging restarts.
This is separate from the broader Scheduling Preferences concept.

\section*{Sets and Indices}
% ===================================================================
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathcal{J} = \{1,\dots,J\}$ -- weekly candidate job pool (after exclusion filters and upstream pre-filtering to eligible Work Order jobs only).
    \item $\mathcal{J}^H = \{j \in \mathcal{J} : h_j = 1\}$ -- subset of two-man (helper-needed) jobs.
    \item $\mathcal{T} = \{1,\dots,T\}$ -- available technicians (currently 3 named technicians).
    \item $\mathcal{K} = \{1,\dots,K\}$ -- available vehicles (currently 5: three main V-1/V-3/V-5, one radiator V-2, plus overflow V-4 when \texttt{include\_v4}=true).  These serve \textbf{4 route slots}: 3~technician rows + Van~4 (V-2 replaces a main vehicle on radiator days, not an additional slot).
    \item $\mathcal{D} = \{1,\dots,D\}$ -- working days in the planning week ($D \leq 5$).
    \item $\mathcal{A}$ -- set of geographic areas / zones (identified by normalised two-digit area numbers).
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Helper Model}
\begin{itemize}
    \item The number of helpers available is configurable (current default: 2).
    \item The engine outputs \textbf{helper required = yes/no} per route/day; it does \emph{not} assign specific helpers by name.
    \item If any job on a route requires a helper, the entire route/day is helper-required.
    \item Once a helper is assigned to a technician, they stay together for the whole day.
    \item Boiler maintenance, service calls, and steam system inspections do not require a helper by job type.
    \item Accepted Quotes are treated as always Two-Man in Initial Build.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Route-Capacity Targets}
Two capacity limits apply simultaneously to each (vehicle, day) slot:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Physical capacity} ($Q_k$, rule \texttt{CAPACITY\_OK}): the hard maximum stops the vehicle can physically hold. Differs by vehicle type (e.g.\ 14 for main trucks, 10 for V-4, 8 for V-2).
    \item \textbf{Seasonal booking cap} (rule \texttt{BOOKED\_CAP}): the soft target number of booked jobs per route, set by the current season from Weekly Context:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Winter:} 4~booked + 2~standby per route (possible 5th booked when backlog is high).
        \item \textbf{Summer:} 3~booked + 2~standby per route.
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}
The standby pool is capped at 2~candidates per route (spec~\S5).

\subsection*{Technician Parameters}
Each technician $t$ is characterised by:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Skill set $\mathit{skill}_t$.
    \item Weekly availability after exceptions: $\mathcal{D}_t \subseteq \mathcal{D}$.
    \item Home depot / start location $(y_t, x_t)$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Job Parameters}
Each job $j \in \mathcal{J}$ carries (per the Candidate Jobs field dictionary):
\begin{itemize}
    \item Job Number / Job ID (primary identifier).
    \item Service address with City, State, and geocoordinates $(\mathrm{lat}_j, \mathrm{lon}_j)$.  Preferred source is Airtable; Python geocodes as fallback.
    \item Normalised two-digit Area Number $a_j \in \mathcal{A}$ and human-readable Area Name.
    \item Job Category / Job Type (arrives pre-standardised from ServiceM8).
    \item ServiceM8 Queue (consumed as-is; see Section~\ref{sec:queues}).
    \item Required Job Hours $\tau_j$ when present; else fallback rules apply (Section~\ref{sec:categories}).
    \item Radiator Count and Refinisher Location (radiator jobs only).
    \item Created Date or Computed Job Age $\alpha_j$ (days).
    \item $2\times$ Average Wait Flag (Airtable-managed aging signal).
    \item Rebook Count With Notes / Without Notes (supports rebook-age rule).
    \item Scheduling Preference Detail (when available; source pending).
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Time and Distance}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Drive times / travel-time calculations are always derived fresh at runtime by the engine using a real routing service (e.g.\ Google Maps), not simple distance math.
    \item Geocodes: preferred Airtable-owned; Python fallback if Airtable cannot supply reliably.  If Python cannot geocode an address, the job is skipped with a communication record.
    \item Vehicle capacity: $Q_k$ -- maximum stops per route for vehicle $k$.
    \item Maximum daily route duration: $T_{\max}$ (minutes).
    \item Technicians leave the shop at 7:00\,a.m.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Seasonal Scoring and Ranking}
\label{sec:scoring_model}

The engine uses a short \textbf{decision-ladder} scoring model rather than a large point system.
A job cannot be in two queues at once.
Scoring rules are configurable so NES can tune behaviour after testing.

\paragraph{Scheduling Preferences.}
Scheduling Preferences are handled separately as an early-pass constraint/review layer and are \textbf{not part of queue scoring}.
Jobs in this queue are scored at the same tier as Normal jobs; their preferences are applied as hard constraints (clear preferences) or flagged for Ryan (vague notes), not via a tier boost.

\paragraph{Winter ranking order.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Priority jobs first.
    \item Normal jobs that have crossed the $2\times$-average-wait threshold.
    \item Accepted Quotes and Requested Scheduling (equal tier; better route wins).
    \item Normal jobs last (including Scheduling Preferences jobs that passed preference checks).
\end{enumerate}
Within winter ranking, aging matters and older jobs are preferred, but route/geography must still make reasonable sense.
A $2\times$-average job does not justify absurd isolation or nonsense geography.

\paragraph{Summer ranking order.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Priority jobs first.
    \item Accepted Quotes and Requested Scheduling (equal tier; route quality decides).
    \item Normal jobs last (including Scheduling Preferences jobs that passed preference checks).
\end{enumerate}
In summer, aging is \emph{not} an active scoring factor for normal route selection.
A $2\times$-average Normal job does not override Requested Scheduling in summer and does not beat a younger Normal job on aging alone.

\subsection*{Geographic Flexibility and Route-Shape Rules}
\label{sec:geography}

The engine judges geography using both local proximity and full route shape, with \textbf{full route shape mattering most}.
Providence shop is an explicit routing factor in Initial Build.

\begin{itemize}
    \item Acceptable route shapes: clusters, sensible lines/corridors, and Providence-assisted shapes.
    \item On-the-way jobs not near the cluster may be included only as \textbf{first-only} or \textbf{last-only} jobs.
    \item First-only / last-only always applies; queue status does not override it.
    \item Line/corridor routes are acceptable only when they flow sensibly without zigzagging.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Implementation status.}
The current code uses \textbf{depot-to-job haversine distance} as a proxy for geographic quality.
Full route-shape analysis (cluster tightness, corridor flow, zigzag detection), per-queue flexibility differentiation, and Providence-shop-as-routing-factor are spec requirements that map to the deferred \texttt{ROUTE\_SHAPE\_OK} and \texttt{POSITION\_OK} eligibility rules.
The geography-flexibility table below documents the \emph{target} behaviour; the code applies uniform proximity today.

\paragraph{Geography flexibility by queue / trigger.}

\medskip
{\small
\begin{longtable}{lp{3.5cm}p{6cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{Job class / trigger} & \textbf{Geography flexibility} & \textbf{Practical meaning} \\
\midrule
Normal jobs           & Normal geography only & Use sensible cluster or sensible line/corridor; do not weaken just because capacity is tight. \\
Requested Scheduling  & Moderate & Can flex beyond normal geography, but route still has to make practical sense. \\
Priority              & Substantial & May justify substantially weaker geography and even fragile routes. \\
Accepted Quotes       & Substantial & Long-duration jobs make geography less important; fragile route allowed with anchor-hold. \\
$2\times$-average Normal (winter override) & Can outrank AQ / RS in winter & Route must still make reasonable sense.  Do not use this override for absurd isolation or nonsense geography.  Trigger source still unresolved. \\
Scheduling Preferences & Moderate if needed & Hard preference constraints still control eligibility; do not force a fragile/underfilled day just because preference matched. \\
\bottomrule
\end{longtable}
}

\paragraph{Seasonal emphasis.}
Summer emphasises best/most efficient geography.
Winter gives aging more weight, so clusters and sensible lines are both acceptable based on aging pressure.
When winter aging and geography are in tension, prefer the older job even if geography is somewhat worse.

\paragraph{Winter emergency coverage.}
In winter, always aim to keep one vehicle in the Boston metro area (Areas 9, 10, 11, 14 are interchangeable for Boston coverage).
Providence coverage is desirable but lower priority because all vehicles return toward Providence anyway.

\subsection*{Weekly Exceptions}
Before route construction the engine applies weekly exceptions (the authoritative source of within-week availability reduction):
\begin{itemize}
    \item Full technician-day blocks (Initial Build supports full-day blocks only).
    \item Vehicle unavailability.
    \item Pre-booked capacity that consumes partial route slots.
\end{itemize}
These reduce effective weekly capacity before the scheduling layer begins.

\subsection*{Validation and Fallback Behaviour}
\begin{itemize}
    \item If a job is missing geocodes, Python geocodes it as fallback.  If that also fails, the job is skipped.
    \item If Required Job Hours is missing, Python uses the defined fallback rules (Section~\ref{sec:categories}) rather than skipping the job.
    \item If another required non-hour field is missing, the job is skipped.
    \item Skipped jobs are surfaced through the communication flow (Job~\# and what needs fixing). No standalone validation report is needed.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Duplicate--Address Handling}
\label{sec:duplicates}

Duplicate policing primarily happens upstream in Airtable before Python scheduling begins.
Python is \textbf{not} expected to be the main duplicate-detection system for Initial Build.
If a suspected duplicate still slips through and materially affects route construction, Python surfaces it through Pre-Route or Route Communications.
Where duplicate review is still needed in Python fallback scenarios, the flag should be minimal: \textbf{Job~\# and what needs fixing}.

\paragraph{Radiator replacement slave job exception.}
Multiple radiator replacement slave jobs at 15 Monticello, Providence must \textbf{never} be treated as duplicates.
That address is the storage facility and multiple same-day pickup jobs there are expected.
The engine continues to flag probable duplicates for Ryan review when duplicate jobs are detected by matching property address, except for the radiator slave job exception above.

\section*{Planning Pipeline}
\label{sec:pipeline}
% ===================================================================

The weekly planning pipeline runs the following stages in order.
Every stage is a deterministic function of its inputs; there is no randomness and no external solver.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Load \& Validate} -- Import the four input payloads.  Run validation checks (Section~\ref{sec:validation}).  Skip jobs with unfixable missing data; surface skipped jobs through communications.
    \item \textbf{Apply Weekly Exceptions} -- Subtract technician-day blocks and vehicle unavailability.  Compute the effective availability sets $\mathcal{D}_t$, $\mathcal{D}_k$.
    \item \textbf{Reserve Special Routes} -- Place Radiator Runs (require V-2), NH Overnight Runs, and helper-needed routes \emph{before} normal filling.
    \item \textbf{Phase~1: Queue-Based Scoring \& Scheduling (Section~\ref{sec:scheduling})} -- Apply the seasonal decision-ladder ranking; walk the sorted list and assign each job to the best eligible (technician, vehicle, day) slot.
    \item \textbf{Phase~2: Order Stops (Section~\ref{sec:routing})} -- For each day's assigned job set, arrange stops in geographic order using a nearest-neighbour heuristic.  Verify time and distance feasibility.
    \item \textbf{Select Standby Jobs} -- Choose the two lowest-priority acceptable candidates per route as standby.
    \item \textbf{Compute Area Readiness} -- Derive area communication layer (\texttt{Good}\,/\,\texttt{Moderate}\,/\,\texttt{Lean}).  Ownership note: if Airtable can calculate it, Airtable should.
    \item \textbf{Generate Communications} -- Emit Pre-Route Communications for week-build issues.
    \item \textbf{Render Outputs} -- Route maps, flags, transcription calendar, reason codes.  Exclusion reasons are surfaced as Pre-Route Communications (spec~\S14.2).
\end{enumerate}

% ===================================================================
\section*{Phase~1: Queue-Based Weekly Scheduling}
\label{sec:scheduling}
% ===================================================================

The scheduling phase decides \emph{which} jobs to serve and \emph{who does them, with which vehicle, on which day}.
It uses the seasonal decision-ladder ranking (Section~\ref{sec:scoring_model}) followed by a deterministic assign loop: no solver, no randomness, fully explainable.

\subsection*{Step 1 -- Queue-Based Ranking}

Jobs in the weekly candidate pool are ordered by the decision-ladder model rather than a single composite score.
The engine first groups jobs by queue tier (see Section~\ref{sec:queues}), then applies seasonal ranking:

\paragraph{Winter.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Priority jobs (highest).
    \item Normal jobs that have crossed the $2\times$-average-wait threshold.
    \item Accepted Quotes and Requested Scheduling (equal tier; the job that produces the better route wins).
    \item Normal jobs (lowest).
\end{enumerate}
Within each tier, older jobs are preferred, but geography must still make reasonable sense.

\paragraph{Summer.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Priority jobs (highest).
    \item Accepted Quotes and Requested Scheduling (equal tier; route quality decides).
    \item Normal jobs (lowest).
\end{enumerate}
Aging is not an active tiebreaker for normal route selection in summer.

\paragraph{Scheduling Preferences pass.}
Scheduling Preferences are handled as an early-pass constraint/review layer \emph{after} anchor/priority jobs and \emph{before} normal jobs.
Clear preferences (e.g.\ ``Tuesday or Thursday mornings'') are hard rules.
Vague or unclear notes are flagged for Ryan and not scheduled until clarified.

\subsection*{Step 2 -- Eligibility Check}

For each candidate assignment of job $j$ to technician $t$, vehicle $k$, day $d$, the engine evaluates a named eligibility checklist.
The assignment is \textbf{eligible} only if \emph{every} rule passes:

\medskip
{\small
\begin{longtable}{lp{9.5cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{Rule name} & \textbf{Condition} \\
\midrule
\texttt{TECH\_AVAILABLE}     & Technician $t$ is available on day $d$ after exceptions: $d \in \mathcal{D}_t$. \\
\texttt{VEH\_AVAILABLE}      & Vehicle $k$ is available on day $d$ after exceptions: $d \in \mathcal{D}_k$. \\
\texttt{VEH\_WORK\_ELIGIBLE} & Vehicle $k$ is allowed to carry the job's work type (e.g.\ V-4 cannot carry Accepted Quotes or helper-required routes). \\
\texttt{SKILL\_MATCH}        & Technician skills cover job requirements: $\mathit{skill}_t \supseteq \text{req}(j)$. \\
\texttt{ROUTE\_SHAPE\_OK}    & Job fits the route's geographic shape (cluster, corridor, or Providence-assisted); on-the-way jobs accepted only as first-only or last-only. \\
\texttt{CAPACITY\_OK}        & Adding this job would not exceed the route-capacity target for the current season. \\
\texttt{TIME\_OK}            & Adding this job's planned hours would not push the day's total past $T_{\max}$, accounting for travel time from the routing service. \\
\texttt{POSITION\_OK}        & First-only / last-only position constraints are satisfiable given currently accepted booking windows. \\
\texttt{ONE\_VEH\_PER\_TECH} & Technician $t$ has not already been assigned a different vehicle on day $d$. \\
\texttt{ONE\_TECH\_PER\_VEH} & Vehicle $k$ has not already been assigned to a different technician on day $d$. \\
\bottomrule
\end{longtable}
}

If every rule passes, the assignment is feasible.  If any rule fails, the engine records which rule failed and moves to the next candidate slot.

\subsection*{Step 3 -- Slot Selection (Best-Fit Assignment)}

For each job (in ranked order), the engine considers all eligible (technician, vehicle, day) slots and picks the \textbf{best fit} using a preference ranking:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Closest depot (proximity proxy for route shape).} Prefer the slot whose technician depot is nearest to the job.  This serves as a proxy for route-shape quality until the full \texttt{ROUTE\_SHAPE\_OK} rule is implemented; see Deferred Items.
    \item \textbf{Lightest day.} Among comparable proximity, prefer the day with the fewest stops already assigned.
    \item \textbf{Geography flexibility.} Apply the queue-specific flexibility level (see Section~\ref{sec:geography}): Normal jobs and Scheduling Preferences get normal geography only; Priority and Accepted Quotes allow substantial weakening; Requested Scheduling allows moderate weakening.  \emph{Note:} the current code applies uniform proximity for all queues; per-queue flexibility differentiation is a planned enhancement.
\end{enumerate}

If no eligible slot exists, the job is recorded with the first failing rule as the reason code.

\subsection*{Step 4 -- Helper Flagging (Two-Man Jobs)}

For every assigned job $j \in \mathcal{J}^H$ (helper-needed) or any Accepted Quote (treated as always Two-Man), the engine marks the entire route/day as \textbf{helper-required = yes}.
The engine does \emph{not} assign specific helpers by name---it only outputs the flag.
Once a helper is assigned to a technician, they stay together for the whole day.

\subsection*{Step 5 -- Workload Review}

After all jobs are assigned, the engine checks workload balance:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Compute each technician's total weekly service time.
    \item If any technician exceeds 90\,\% of their available hours, emit a \texttt{TECH\_OVERLOAD} flag.
    \item If any vehicle is at 100\,\% capacity on 4+ days, emit a \texttt{VEH\_BOTTLENECK} flag.
\end{itemize}

No jobs are moved or swapped at this stage---the flags exist so Ryan can decide whether the imbalance is acceptable.

\subsection*{Algorithm Summary}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Queue-Based Weekly Scheduling (Phase~1)}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Candidate jobs $\mathcal{J}$, technicians $\mathcal{T}$, vehicles $\mathcal{K}$, days $\mathcal{D}$, season, config
\Ensure Assignments list, standby list, exclusion list, review flags
\State Apply seasonal decision-ladder ranking to $\mathcal{J}$
\State Process Scheduling Preferences as early-pass constraints
\For{each job $j$ in ranked order}
    \State $\text{best\_slot} \gets \textsc{None}$
    \For{each $(t, k, d)$ in eligible slots for $j$}
        \State Check all eligibility rules
        \If{all rules pass \textbf{and} slot is better than $\text{best\_slot}$}
            \State $\text{best\_slot} \gets (t, k, d)$
        \EndIf
    \EndFor
    \If{$\text{best\_slot}$ found}
        \State Assign $j \to \text{best\_slot}$; update capacity trackers
        \If{$j \in \mathcal{J}^H$ or $j$ is Accepted Quote}
            \State Mark route/day as helper-required
        \EndIf
    \Else
        \State Record exclusion: $(j, \text{first failing rule})$
    \EndIf
\EndFor
\State Select 2 standby jobs per route (lowest-priority acceptable candidates)
\State Run workload-review checks; emit flags
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Why this approach.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Explainable:} every assignment can be traced to ``job $j$ scored $X$; slot $(t,k,d)$ was chosen because it was the closest eligible depot on the lightest day.''
    \item \textbf{Deterministic:} same inputs always produce the same plan.
    \item \textbf{Adjustable:} the tier order and tiebreaker ratios (winter: $0.6\,\text{age}+0.4\,\text{geo}$; summer: $0.7\,\text{geo}+0.3\,\text{age}$) are designed to be configurable.  Initial Build hardcodes defaults that match the spec ranking; config-file injection is a planned enhancement.
    \item \textbf{No solver dependency:} pure Python, no Pyomo, no CBC, no external libraries beyond standard data-processing.
    \item \textbf{Upgrade path:} if the greedy approach leaves too much value on the table, Phase~1 can be replaced with an optimisation model (MILP or constraint programming) in Version~2 without changing the rest of the pipeline.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Phase~2: Stop Ordering (Nearest--Neighbour Routing)}
\label{sec:routing}
% ===================================================================

After Phase~1 assigns jobs to (technician, vehicle, day) bundles, Phase~2 arranges the stops within each day into a drivable geographic sequence.
Initial Build uses a simple, explainable heuristic---no VRP solver.

\subsection*{Step 1 -- Build the Day's Stop List}

For each (technician $t$, vehicle $k$, day $d$) bundle, collect the assigned jobs $\mathcal{J}_{t,k,d}$.
The depot (technician start location) is node~0.

\subsection*{Step 2 -- Nearest--Neighbour Sequencing}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Nearest--Neighbour Stop Ordering}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Depot $(y_t, x_t)$, assigned jobs $\mathcal{J}_{t,k,d}$, distance function $d(\cdot,\cdot)$
\Ensure Ordered route $\Omega$
\State $\text{current} \gets \text{depot}$
\State $\text{remaining} \gets \mathcal{J}_{t,k,d}$
\State $\Omega \gets [\text{depot}]$
\While{remaining $\neq \emptyset$}
    \State $j^* \gets \arg\min_{j \in \text{remaining}} d(\text{current}, j)$
    \State Append $j^*$ to $\Omega$; remove $j^*$ from remaining
    \State $\text{current} \gets j^*$
\EndWhile
\State Append depot to $\Omega$ \Comment{return to start}
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Initial Build computes inter-stop distances via Haversine (straight-line), which is sufficient for stop \emph{sequencing}.
Note: the spec (\S3) requires drive-time calculations from a real routing service (e.g.\ Google Maps) for \emph{travel-time budgeting}; the Haversine here is the sequencing heuristic only.
Road-network distances can be substituted without changing the algorithm.

\subsection*{Step 3 -- Feasibility Verification}

After sequencing, the engine walks the route and checks:

\medskip
{\small
\begin{longtable}{lp{9cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{Check} & \textbf{Action if violated} \\
\midrule
Total travel + service time $\leq T_{\max}$ & Drop the last-added job from the route; re-check.  Dropped job gets reason code \texttt{ROUTE\_DROP}. \\
Number of stops $\leq Q_k$ & Should not happen (enforced in Phase~1), but drop excess if it does. \\
Inter-stop distance $\leq r$ & Flag the arc as \texttt{CROSS\_AREA}; do not drop (guidance, not hard wall). \\
Every stop within radius $R$ of depot & Should not happen (eligibility enforced), but flag \texttt{GEOCODE\_OOB} if violated. \\
\bottomrule
\end{longtable}
}

If any job is dropped, the engine re-runs the nearest-neighbour on the reduced set so the sequence stays sensible.

\subsection*{Why nearest-neighbour (not a solver)}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Transparent:} the route order is ``go to the closest un-visited stop.''  No parameter tuning, no black-box behaviour.
    \item \textbf{Fast:} $O(n^2)$ for $n$ stops.  For typical NES clusters ($n \leq 14$), this is instantaneous.
    \item \textbf{Good enough:} nearest-neighbour typically delivers routes within 20--25\,\% of the optimal tour length.  For Initial Build the priority is correctness and reviewability, not distance optimality.
    \item \textbf{Upgrade path:} if route quality matters (fuel costs, tight schedules), a 2-opt improvement pass or a proper TSP/VRP solver can be added in a future version as a drop-in replacement.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Hard Constraints Summary}
% ===================================================================

The following rules are enforced throughout the pipeline.  None may be relaxed; every violation either blocks the assignment or triggers a job exclusion with a reason code.
The engine evaluates \textbf{12 named eligibility rules} for every candidate (job, technician, vehicle, day) slot:

\medskip
{\small
\begin{longtable}{lp{9cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{Rule Name} & \textbf{Description} \\
\midrule
\texttt{TECH\_AVAILABLE}    & Technician must be available on the planned day (after weekly exceptions). \\
\texttt{VEH\_AVAILABLE}     & Vehicle must be available on the planned day (after weekly exceptions). \\
\texttt{SKILL\_MATCH}       & Technician skills must cover the job's requirements. \\
\texttt{VEH\_CAPABILITY}    & Vehicle capability tags must cover the job's requirements. \\
\texttt{VEH\_WORK\_ELIGIBLE}& V-4 may carry \emph{only} Steam System Inspections, Service Calls, and Boiler Maintenance. Main vehicles have no category restriction. \\
\texttt{WITHIN\_RADIUS}     & Haversine distance from technician depot to job must not exceed $R$ (cluster radius, default 30\,km). \\
\texttt{NOT\_PROHIBITED}    & (technician, job) pair must not be in the prohibited-pairs set. \\
\texttt{CAPACITY\_OK}       & Physical vehicle capacity $Q_k$ must not be exceeded for the (vehicle, day) slot. \\
\texttt{BOOKED\_CAP}        & Seasonal booking target must not be exceeded (winter: 4, summer: 3, from Weekly Context). \\
\texttt{TIME\_OK}           & Daily service-time budget $T_{\max} \times \text{phase1\_fraction}$ must not be exceeded. \\
\texttt{ONE\_VEH\_PER\_TECH}& Each technician uses at most one vehicle per day. \\
\texttt{ONE\_TECH\_PER\_VEH}& Each vehicle is assigned to at most one technician per day. \\
\bottomrule
\end{longtable}
}

\noindent Additional structural constraints (enforced by the pipeline but not as per-slot eligibility checks):
\begin{itemize}
    \item Helper-required routes must be flagged; once a helper is on a route, they stay for the whole day.
    \item No job is assigned more than once.
    \item V-2 is used for radiator days only.
    \item Standby pool is capped at 2~candidates per route.
\end{itemize}

\noindent \textbf{Deferred:} \texttt{ROUTE\_SHAPE\_OK} (geographic-shape fit) and \texttt{POSITION\_OK} (first-only/last-only) are spec requirements not yet implemented as eligibility rules; they are listed in Deferred Items.

Each constraint maps directly to a named rule in the eligibility-check table (Phase~1, Step~2) or the feasibility-verification table (Phase~2, Step~3).

\section*{Special Route Types}
% ===================================================================

Special routes are planned \emph{before} normal route filling to reserve capacity:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Radiator Runs} -- Require Vehicle~2 (V-2).  V-2 must not be used as a normal standard route in Initial Build.  The Airtable radiator-hours table supplies planned-hours values.
    \item \textbf{NH Overnight Runs} -- Default structured overnight/Two-Man route.  Normal age/queue/fairness logic still applies.  A one-day NH-border exception is allowed and may be one-man.
    \item \textbf{Two-Man / Helper Routes} -- Jobs flagged as helper-required, plus all Accepted Quotes (treated as always Two-Man in Initial Build).  The entire route/day becomes helper-required if any job on it needs a helper.
\end{enumerate}

After special routes consume capacity, the remaining capacity feeds normal scheduling.

\section*{Solution Approach}
% ===================================================================

Initial Build uses a single, deterministic algorithm for each phase:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Phase~1 (Scheduling):} Score--sort--assign greedy loop with 12 named eligibility rules (Section~\ref{sec:scheduling}).
    \item \textbf{Phase~2 (Routing):} Nearest--neighbour stop ordering with post-hoc feasibility checks (Section~\ref{sec:routing}).
\end{itemize}

No external solver, optimisation library, or metaheuristic is required.
The engine completes a full weekly plan for the current fleet size (26 jobs, 3 technicians, 5 vehicles, 5 days) in under one second on commodity hardware.

If route quality improvements are needed in a future version, a 2-opt improvement pass or a TSP/VRP solver can be introduced as a drop-in replacement for the nearest-neighbour step without changing the rest of the pipeline.

\section*{Anchor, Standby, Replacement, and Day-Recovery Logic}
\label{sec:standby}
% ===================================================================

\subsection*{Standby Job Selection}

After route construction, the engine selects two standby jobs per route---the lowest-priority jobs among otherwise acceptable route candidates.
Standby jobs must be distinguished from assigned jobs in the engine's outputs; each assignment carries an explicit \texttt{role} tag (\texttt{assigned}, \texttt{standby}, or \texttt{anchor\_hold}).

If enough valid standby jobs do not exist, the route normally should not run unless work is thin and Ryan is expected to work manual adjustments afterward.
The engine surfaces this condition through a \texttt{WEAK\_STANDBY} review flag (severity WARN) on any route with fewer than two standby candidates.

\subsection*{Anchor-Hold Logic}

For fragile routes---typically those built around long-duration Accepted Quotes, Priority jobs, or Requested Scheduling jobs with weak geography---the engine may designate an \textbf{anchor-hold job}: a high-value job that justifies the route's existence even if the supporting geography is thin.
Per the queue treatment table (Section~\ref{sec:queues}): Priority \emph{may serve as anchor}; Accepted Quotes may use anchor-hold on fragile routes; Requested Scheduling \emph{may serve as anchor}.
Anchor-hold should generally \emph{not} be used in Replacement Route (adds too much time), except when jobs are thin and it is the only workable option.

\paragraph{Implementation status.}
Anchor-hold designation is not yet implemented in code.
The standby-selection module identifies standby candidates, but the anchor-hold flag (marking a job as the route's justification) is a planned enhancement.

\subsection*{Day-Damage Recovery (Ryan-Initiated)}

The engine supports two Ryan-initiated day-recovery workflows in Initial Build.
These are \emph{not} automatic background behaviours; they are triggered by Ryan from the post-sync single-route / single-day / single-technician view.

\paragraph{Replacement Job.}
Launched when a mostly intact route loses one job.
Replacement Job is launched from the post-sync single-route / single-day / single-technician view.
If easiest to build, Airtable may send the whole route view.
The engine uses that context---current jobs, accepted jobs, and accepted booking windows---when searching for a replacement.

\begin{itemize}
    \item Return up to two good replacement candidates when they exist, unranked (both may be invited).
    \item If only one good candidate exists, return only that candidate.
    \item If no good candidate exists, return the best available candidate marked as ``outside normal parameters.''
    \item An on-the-way first-only / last-only candidate is valid only if the already accepted booking windows leave a workable first or last slot.  If that slot is not available, the candidate must be rejected and the engine should keep searching.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Replacement Route (Blow-Up / Rebuild).}
Launched when a day must be entirely rebuilt for the same technician/route.
The rebuilt route includes standby jobs like a normal new route and actively reconsiders the weakened day rather than just swapping one job.

If a rebuilt route is workable but weak, the engine still emits the best workable result and surfaces the warning through Route Communications rather than suppressing the route.

\paragraph{Implementation status.}
Replacement Job and Replacement Route are defined as callable workflows (Section~\ref{sec:workflows}) but are not yet implemented in code.
The Weekly Run workflow (score-and-assign pipeline) is fully implemented; the two recovery workflows are planned enhancements that will reuse the same eligibility, scoring, and slot-selection modules.

\section*{Booking-Window and Feasibility Logic}
\label{sec:windows}
% ===================================================================

The engine is not responsible for full downstream invitation management, but it must output route-position constraints and basic timing information that affects invitation choice.

\subsection*{Window Rules}

Technicians depart the shop at 7:00\,a.m.  The \emph{estimated arrival} at the first stop is 7:00 plus the travel time from the shop to that stop's address (computed via the routing service).  This arrival time determines the first window:

\medskip
{\small
\begin{tabular}{llp{6cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{Slot} & \textbf{Window} & \textbf{Rule / pairing} \\
\midrule
First stop (early)  & 7--8   & Used when estimated arrival is before 8:00.  Implies \textbf{8--11} as the second window. \\
First stop (late)   & 8--9   & Used when estimated arrival is 8:00 or later.  Implies \textbf{10--1} as the second window. \\
Third stop          & 11--2  & Fixed sequence after first two windows. \\
Fourth stop         & 1--4   & Fixed sequence. \\
Optional fifth stop & 2--5   & Used only when a fifth booked job is added. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
}

\medskip
The engine must output first-only and last-only flags when route shape requires them.

\subsection*{Feasibility / Conflict Flagging}

Initial Build supports practical feasibility flagging:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Detect obviously impossible route/window combinations.
    \item Flag likely renegotiation needs.
    \item Identify when a route probably cannot be served with the currently accepted windows.
\end{itemize}
The engine should \emph{not} become a full invitation-window planner.

\subsection*{Related Sub-Topics}

The consolidated spec (\S8) groups two additional sub-topics under booking-window logic:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Radiator-hour source and radiator-route rules} (\S8.1) -- covered in Section~\ref{sec:categories} (Job-Category Planned Hours) and the radiator-hours notes in Deferred Items.
    \item \textbf{Duplicate-job handling} (\S8.2) -- covered in Section~\ref{sec:duplicates} (Duplicate--Address Handling).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Implementation status.}
Booking-window assignment and feasibility flagging are not yet implemented in code.
The current pipeline enforces time-budget constraints (\texttt{TIME\_OK}) but does not assign specific window slots (7--8, 8--9, etc.) or detect window conflicts.
This is a planned enhancement; the data model, communication records, and acceptance criteria are defined above.

\section*{Area Readiness}

The engine computes an area--readiness indicator for each geographic area $a \in \mathcal{A}$:

\[
\text{Readiness}(a) \in \{\texttt{Good},\;\texttt{Moderate},\;\texttt{Lean}\}

based on the density and quality of planned activity in that area.

\section*{Route Map Rendering}
\label{sec:mapping}
% ===================================================================

The engine generates a per-route PNG map for every (technician, vehicle, day) bundle.
These maps are the primary visual review artefact: they let the owner confirm geographic sanity at a glance without reading coordinate tables.

\subsection*{Implementation}

Map rendering is implemented in \texttt{nes\_dispatch/mapping.py} using OSMnx, NetworkX, and Matplotlib.
For each route the pipeline:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Collects all stop coordinates (depot + job sites) and computes the geographic centre and bounding radius.
    \item Downloads a road-network graph from OpenStreetMap via \texttt{osmnx.graph\_from\_point(centre, dist=radius)}. Per-route graphs are small (typically 500--2\,000 nodes) and download in seconds.
    \item Snaps each coordinate to the nearest road-network node using brute-force search over graph nodes (avoids a scikit-learn dependency).
    \item Computes road-following paths between consecutive stops via \texttt{networkx.shortest\_path} (Dijkstra on the road graph).
    \item Renders the map with Matplotlib: light-gray road network on a dark background, coloured route segments (one CSS4 colour per leg), a red square for the depot, and green circles for job stops.
\end{enumerate}

OSMnx disk caching is enabled so that repeated runs for the same service area do not re-download road data.
A retry mechanism with exponential backoff handles transient Overpass API failures.

\section*{Owner--Controlled Policy Inputs}
% ===================================================================

The engine exposes a compact set of tunable parameters that a non-technical business owner can adjust--via Airtable fields or a configuration file--to steer planning behaviour without modifying code.

\paragraph{Maximum daily route duration ($T_{\max}$).}
Caps the total travel-plus-service time for any single route.
Lowering $T_{\max}$ produces shorter days and leaves more buffer for delays; raising it allows more jobs per day but increases overtime risk.

\paragraph{Maximum stops per route ($P$) and vehicle capacities ($Q_k$).}
$P$ is a global upper bound; each vehicle's capacity $Q_k \leq P$ can be set independently.
Reducing $Q_k$ for a specific vehicle reserves slack for ad-hoc stops or equipment space.

\paragraph{Cluster radius ($R$) and inter-stop limit ($r$).}
$R$ defines how far from a technician's home depot jobs may be included; $r$ limits the maximum gap between consecutive stops.
Tightening these values produces geographically compact routes with shorter drive legs; relaxing them allows the engine to reach more jobs at the expense of longer routes.

\paragraph{Seasonal decision-ladder configuration.}
The engine selects the active tier order and tiebreaker weights based on the season supplied in Weekly Context (Winter or Summer).
Within each tier, winter emphasises job age (older jobs preferred) while summer emphasises geography (route quality preferred).
Tier boundaries and tiebreaker ratios are configurable so NES can tune behaviour after testing.

\paragraph{Weekly exceptions.}
Technician and vehicle unavailability entries (PTO, maintenance, pre-booked installs) entered each week.
These directly shrink the effective capacity seen by Phase~1 and must be finalised before the planning run.

\paragraph{Special-route reservations.}
The owner marks which jobs require Radiator Run (V-2), NH Overnight, or helper-needed handling.
Reserved routes are locked before normal filling, so the owner controls how much capacity is set aside for these categories.

\paragraph{Prohibited pairings and area definitions.}
Technician--job exclusions (e.g.\ customer-requested) and the geographic area map ($\mathcal{A}$) are maintained in Airtable.
Changes take effect on the next planning run without engine modification.

\section*{Major Risks and Ambiguities}
% ===================================================================

\paragraph{Phase-1 / Phase-2 feasibility gap.}
Phase~1 budgets planned hours using the routing-service travel times, but real-world conditions may differ.
A conservative $T_{\max}$ buffer (e.g.\ 80\,\% of the true day length) should be used until an iterative feedback loop is added.

\paragraph{Drive-time accuracy.}
Travel times are computed at runtime via a real routing service (e.g.\ Google Maps).
If the routing service returns inaccurate results for rural New England roads, route durations may be mis-estimated.

\paragraph{Greedy scheduling quality.}
The queue-based decision-ladder is deterministic but greedy: it processes jobs in ranked order and cannot backtrack.
A high-tier job may consume a slot that would have been better used by multiple lower-tier jobs.
Monitoring unassigned-job counts and fill-rate variance will reveal whether iterative improvement (swap passes) is needed.

\paragraph{Geocoding accuracy.}
Preferred source is Airtable-owned coordinates; Python geocodes as fallback.
Incorrect or ambiguous addresses produce wrong cluster assignments and distance estimates.
Jobs failing geocoding are skipped with a communication record.

\paragraph{Exception staleness.}
Weekly exceptions must be entered before the planning run.
Late-arriving changes (same-day sick leave) are not captured and require manual adjustment or a re-run.

\paragraph{Seasonal-weight calibration.}
The scoring ranking rules are configurable, but until job-outcome data is available for back-testing, they should be reviewed periodically and adjusted when Ryan consistently overrides recommendations.

\paragraph{Single-week planning horizon.}
Each run plans one week in isolation.
Jobs deferred this week re-enter the pool next week with higher aging, but there is no explicit multi-week look-ahead to prevent systematic starvation.

\paragraph{Radiator-hours tuning.}
Ryan still needs to set and tune the first real values in the Airtable radiator-hours table.
Until those are calibrated, radiator route timing may be inaccurate.

\paragraph{Superseded prior assumptions.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Current status is not a required engine input.
    \item The engine talks in terms of assigned jobs, standby jobs, and anchor-hold jobs---not vague ``backup jobs.''
    \item Scheduling Preferences are not the same as the rebook-age rule.
    \item Vehicle~4 is not a full equivalent route slot; it is a lighter overflow route with work-type restrictions.
    \item Pre-existing calendar load is represented through Weekly Exceptions, not live ServiceM8 calendar access.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Recommended Engine Structure}
\label{sec:engine_structure}
% ===================================================================

The Python engine is organised as a pipeline of self-contained stages, each with explicit inputs, outputs, and a single responsibility.
The recommended package layout is:

\begin{verbatim}
nes_dispatch/
    __init__.py
    data/
        loaders.py          # CSV / Airtable readers -> dataclasses
        models.py           # Job, Technician, Vehicle, WeeklyException, WeeklyData
        validators.py       # Pre-run sanity checks (coordinates, missing fields)
    rules/
        scoring.py          # Composite priority-score formula
        eligibility.py      # 12 named eligibility rules -> pass/fail per slot
        slot_selection.py   # Best-fit ranking (closest depot, lightest day, cheapest vehicle)
        helpers.py          # Helper-assignment secondary pass
        workload.py         # Workload-review flags (no job movement)
    routing/
        distance.py         # Haversine distance computation
        nearest_neighbour.py# NN stop ordering + feasibility verification
    postprocess/
        standby.py          # Standby job ranking
        readiness.py        # Area readiness computation
        flags.py            # Review flags (9 codes, 3 severity levels)
    mapping.py              # Route map rendering (OSMnx + Matplotlib)
    config.py               # Loads JSON/YAML config; merges Airtable overrides
    pipeline.py             # Top-level orchestrator (the 9-step pipeline)
\end{verbatim}

\paragraph{Design principles.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Stateless stages.} Each stage is a pure function: it receives typed dataclass inputs and returns typed outputs. No global mutable state.
    \item \textbf{Fail-fast validation.} \texttt{validators.py} runs before the pipeline and rejects runs with missing columns, out-of-bounds coordinates, or conflicting exceptions. Errors are collected, not thrown one at a time.
    \item \textbf{Named rules.} Every scheduling decision is traceable to a named rule (e.g.\ \texttt{SKILL\_MATCH}, \texttt{CAPACITY\_OK}) and the inputs that triggered it. No opaque scoring or black-box behaviour.
    \item \textbf{Idempotent runs.} Given the same inputs and config, the engine produces identical outputs. The algorithm is fully deterministic--no random seeds, no stochastic components.
    \item \textbf{Separation of I/O.} File reads live in \texttt{data/loaders.py}; map rendering lives in \texttt{mapping.py}. Core logic never touches the filesystem directly.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Pipeline orchestration (\texttt{pipeline.py}).}
The top-level function \texttt{run\_weekly\_plan(config\_path, data\_dir)} executes the nine stages listed in Section~\ref{sec:pipeline} in sequence.
Each stage logs its runtime and emits a structured summary (JSON) so that failures can be diagnosed without re-running the full pipeline.

\paragraph{Testing strategy.}
Unit tests cover individual stages with small synthetic fixtures.
An integration test runs the full pipeline on the example CSVs and asserts that:
\begin{itemize}
    \item every assigned job satisfies all 12 eligibility rules;
    \item no technician exceeds $T_{\max}$;
    \item every helper-needed job has \texttt{helper\_required=True} on its assignment;
    \item output files are generated without errors.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Rationale.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Code} holds logic that requires developer review to change--model structure, algorithm implementations, output formats. Changes go through version control and testing.
    \item \textbf{Config (JSON/YAML)} holds numerical parameters that a developer or technically confident owner adjusts between runs. The config file is version-controlled alongside the code so that every run is reproducible. A schema validator rejects unknown keys or out-of-range values before the pipeline starts.
    \item \textbf{Airtable} holds operational data that changes weekly or on demand--rosters, exceptions, pairings, area maps. The owner edits these freely; the engine pulls the latest state at the start of each run via the Airtable API. No code deployment is needed.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Override precedence.}
When the same parameter appears in both the config file and an Airtable field (e.g.\ a per-vehicle capacity override), the Airtable value takes precedence.
This lets the owner make one-off adjustments without touching the config file, while the config file provides sensible defaults.

\paragraph{Versioning and audit trail.}
Every planning run records:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The config-file hash (git commit or content hash).
    \item A snapshot of the Airtable tables consumed (written to a timestamped JSON file).
    \item The engine version (Python package version or git SHA).
\end{enumerate}
This triple ensures that any past run can be fully reproduced or audited.

\section*{Validation and Testing Approach}
\label{sec:validation}
% ===================================================================

Correctness is enforced at three levels: input validation (before the pipeline runs), constraint verification (after each layer), and regression testing (during development).

\subsection*{Input Validation (Pre-run)}

\texttt{validators.py} performs the following checks when data is loaded. If any check fails, the run is aborted with a consolidated error report--not a single exception.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Schema completeness.} Every required CSV column is present and non-null for every row.
    \item \textbf{Coordinate bounds.} All latitudes fall within $[41, 48]$ and longitudes within $[-74, -67]$ (approximate New England bounding box). Out-of-range values indicate geocoding errors.
    \item \textbf{Referential integrity.} Every \texttt{scope\_id} in the exceptions file matches an existing technician or vehicle ID. Every \texttt{area\_id} on a job exists in the area definitions.
    \item \textbf{Availability consistency.} After applying exceptions, at least one (technician, vehicle, day) triple must remain feasible; otherwise the run would produce an empty schedule.
    \item \textbf{Duplicate detection.} Duplicate \texttt{job\_id}, \texttt{tech\_id}, or \texttt{vehicle\_id} values are flagged.
    \item \textbf{Config schema.} The JSON/YAML config is validated against a schema: all numerical parameters are present, positive, and within documented bounds (e.g.\ $T_{\max} > 0$, $R > 0$, $P \geq 1$).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Post-Phase Constraint Verification}

After Phase~1 and Phase~2 each complete, the engine runs an assertion suite on the solution before proceeding.

\paragraph{Phase~1 assertions.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Every assigned job appears at most once across all (technician, vehicle, day) triples.
    \item All 12 eligibility rules pass for every assignment.
    \item Vehicle capacity is satisfied per slot: assigned jobs $\leq Q_k$.
    \item Service-time budget holds: total service time $\leq T_{\max}$ per slot.
    \item Each technician uses at most one vehicle per day.
    \item Each vehicle is used by at most one technician per day.
    \item Every helper-needed job has \texttt{helper\_required=True} on its assignment and route.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Phase~2 assertions (per route).}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Route departs from and returns to the depot.
    \item No stop is visited more than once.
    \item Total travel-plus-service time $\leq T_{\max}$.
    \item Number of visited stops $\leq Q_k$.
    \item All stops lie within radius $R$ of the depot.
\end{itemize}

If any assertion fails, the pipeline logs the violation, marks the affected route as \texttt{INFEASIBLE}, and continues with the remaining bundles rather than aborting the entire run.

\subsection*{Regression and Unit Testing}

\paragraph{Unit tests.}
Each module exposes pure functions that can be tested in isolation:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{test\_scoring}: synthetic 5-job scenario; verify decision-ladder tier scores and seasonal tiebreakers match hand-computed values.
    \item \texttt{test\_eligibility}: 3-job, 2-tech, 2-vehicle scenario; verify each of the 12 named rules returns the expected pass/fail.
    \item \texttt{test\_slot\_selection}: given pre-computed eligibility, verify best-fit ranking (closest depot $\to$ lightest day $\to$ cheapest vehicle).
    \item \texttt{test\_nearest\_neighbour}: 5-node instance with known optimal NN tour; compare output sequence.
    \item \texttt{test\_feasibility}: route deliberately exceeding $T_{\max}$; verify job-drop and re-sequence logic.
    \item \texttt{test\_helper}: helper-needed job assigned to route; confirm \texttt{helper\_required=True} flag is set on the assignment and route.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Integration test.}
A single end-to-end test runs the full pipeline on the example CSVs (\texttt{data/example\_*.csv}) and asserts:
\begin{itemize}
    \item no validation errors;
    \item at least one route is generated;
    \item all post-phase assertions pass;
    \item output files (maps, summaries, reason codes) are created and non-empty.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Golden-file comparison.}
Because the engine is fully deterministic (no random components), every run on the same inputs produces identical outputs.
The integration test compares output against a checked-in golden file; a mismatch triggers a diff report so that unintended behavioural changes are caught immediately.

The engine produces a structured set of review flags alongside every weekly plan.
These flags are designed for the non-technical owner to scan quickly and decide whether to approve, adjust, or re-run.

\subsection*{Flag Categories}

Each flag has a severity (\texttt{INFO}, \texttt{WARN}, \texttt{CRITICAL}), a short code, and a human-readable message.

\toprule
\textbf{Code} & \textbf{Severity} & \textbf{Meaning} \\
\midrule

The reason code set is the primary traceability mechanism.

\subsection*{Delivery Mechanism}

Per spec~\S14.2: ``No standalone validation report is needed.  Any skipped jobs should be surfaced through the same communication flow using only Job~\# and what needs fixing.''
Accordingly:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Exclusion reasons} are delivered as \textbf{Pre-Route Communication} records (one per excluded job), with
          \texttt{Communication Type} and severity mapped per the internal-event table in Section~\ref{sec:comms},
          and the \texttt{What Needs Fixing} field populated with the human-readable detail string.
    \item \textbf{Review flags} are routed to Pre-Route or Route Communications depending on scope:
          week-level flags (e.g.\ \texttt{DUP\_ADDR}, \texttt{WEAK\_STANDBY}, \texttt{MISSING\_PLANNED\_HOURS}) become Pre-Route Communications;
          route-specific flags (e.g.\ \texttt{HELPER\_REQUIRED}, \texttt{CROSS\_AREA}) become Route Communications attached to the affected route/day/slot.
    \item \texttt{CRITICAL}-severity communications (``Action Needed'') are additionally surfaced as bold-red banners at the top of the Schedule Week review, ensuring they are not overlooked.
\end{itemize}

The engine retains structured \texttt{ReviewFlag} and \texttt{Exclusion} objects internally for programmatic access, but the \emph{primary} output channel for Ryan is the communication records described in Section~\ref{sec:comms}.
The conversion is implemented by \texttt{generate\_pre\_route\_comms} and \texttt{generate\_route\_comms} in \texttt{nes\_dispatch/postprocess/communications.py}.

\section*{Live-Week Testing and Acceptance}
\label{sec:testing_live}
% ===================================================================

Initial Build testing uses the real upcoming live scheduling week, not an elaborate synthetic test programme.

\begin{itemize}
    \item Ryan may start the week as early as Monday.  If the first output is bad, the freelancer may revise within that week, but only up to a practical Thursday deadline.
    \item If the output is still not usable by Thursday, Ryan reverts to manual scheduling for that week and AI tries again on the next live week.
    \item \textbf{Acceptance standard:} most live weeks should be approved by Ryan with no changes.  One imperfect route does not fail the entire week, but if Ryan is routinely rebuilding, the system has not met the goal.
    \item If two live scheduling weeks fail, the project pauses automatically for review before more implementation continues.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Future Considerations (Beyond Initial Build)}
% ===================================================================

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Iterative Phase-1/Phase-2 feedback:} re-schedule jobs dropped by Phase~2 into alternative slots.
    \item \textbf{2-opt improvement pass:} after nearest-neighbour sequencing, apply pairwise arc swaps to shorten routes.  Low complexity, high payoff.
    \item \textbf{TSP/VRP solver (optional upgrade):} replace the nearest-neighbour step with a proper solver (e.g.\ OR-Tools, Pyomo/CBC) for provably optimal stop ordering.  The rest of the pipeline remains unchanged.
    \item \textbf{MILP scheduling (optional upgrade):} if the greedy scheduler proves too limited, formulate Phase~1 as a mixed-integer program.  The named rules become solver constraints; the upgrade is additive, not a rewrite.
    \item \textbf{Exact appointment-time optimisation:} assign specific arrival windows per stop once route ordering is reliable.
    \item \textbf{Real-time traffic / drive-time API integration:} replace static routing with live data.
    \item \textbf{Historical job-outcome data:} feed completion rates and no-show rates back into the scoring formula.
    \item \textbf{Multi-week rolling horizon planning:} prevent systematic starvation of low-value areas.
    \item \textbf{Generic freeform communication button:} deferred unless testing shows standard buttons are insufficient.
    \item \textbf{Partial-day exceptions:} extend beyond full technician-day blocks.
    \item \textbf{Named helper assignment:} assign specific helpers rather than just flagging helper-required.
\end{itemize}
