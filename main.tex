\section*{1. Purpose}

This document gives a draft mathematical formulation for the Version 1 weekly planning problem described in \texttt{task.pdf}. The goal is not exact stop sequencing or appointment-time booking. Instead, the model formalizes a \emph{route-first weekly scheduling problem} in which the engine selects a set of feasible route-day proposals, assigns them to crews and vehicles, and produces a reviewable weekly draft schedule.

The formulation is intentionally designed to match the business brief:

\begin{itemize}
    \item planning is weekly, not day-by-day;
    \item geography is important but area boundaries are not hard walls;
    \item special route types are handled before normal route filling;
    \item human review remains part of the process;
    \item exact stop order is out of scope for Version 1.
\end{itemize}

\section*{2. Modeling Approach}

We model the planning problem as a \emph{candidate-route set packing and assignment problem}. Before optimization, the engine generates a finite set of feasible route-day candidates from the candidate job pool using geography, route-type rules, and weekly business policy.

Each route candidate already contains:

\begin{itemize}
    \item a day of execution;
    \item a set of jobs that could be grouped together;
    \item a route type (normal, radiator, overnight, helper-needed, etc.);
    \item a geography quality score;
    \item feasibility tags for crews and vehicles.
\end{itemize}

This avoids encoding exact stop sequencing in the optimization model while still preserving route-first planning.

\section*{3. Assumptions}

The draft formulation uses the following assumptions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The weekly candidate pool has already excluded canceled, urgent/manual, already scheduled, and already-in-workflow jobs.
    \item Weekly exceptions for technicians, vehicles, long jobs, and installs are available as structured inputs.
    \item A preprocessing stage generates feasible candidate route-day bundles.
    \item Duplicate addresses are grouped during preprocessing so that route compactness is measured by unique stop groups rather than raw job count.
    \item Special routes are given lexicographic priority over normal route filling.
    \item Area Readiness is derived from selected routes and is not treated as a hard constraint in Version 1.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{4. Notation for Candidate-Route Generation}

The optimization model assumes that the candidate-route set $R$ is generated in preprocessing. This section defines the notation used for that generation step.

\subsection*{4.1 Geographic primitives}

Let $S$ be the set of unique stop groups obtained after duplicate-address consolidation. Each job $j \in J$ belongs to exactly one stop group $s(j) \in S$.

For each stop group $s \in S$ and area $a \in A$, define:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\toprule
Symbol & Meaning \\
\midrule
$\xi_s \in \mathbb{R}^2$ & geographic coordinate or embedded point for stop group $s$ \\
$\operatorname{area}(s) \in A$ & primary business area label of stop group $s$ \\
$d_{ss'} \ge 0$ & geographic distance between stop groups $s$ and $s'$ \\
$\eta_{ss'} \in \{0,1\}$ & adjacency indicator, equal to $1$ if $s'$ is allowed as a geographic neighbor of $s$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The adjacency indicator $\eta_{ss'}$ is not required to respect area boundaries exactly. It may allow cross-area adjacency when the resulting route remains geographically coherent.

\subsection*{4.2 Job-rule indicators}

For each job $j \in J$, define the rule indicators:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\toprule
Symbol & Meaning \\
\midrule
$e_j \in \{0,1\}$ & weekly eligibility indicator after upstream filtering \\
$m_j \in \{0,1\}$ & helper-required indicator \\
$n_j \in \{0,1\}$ & overnight-route indicator \\
$r_j \in \{0,1\}$ & radiator-route indicator \\
$\phi_{jt} \in \{0,1\}$ & compatibility of job $j$ with route type $t$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

These indicators encode business rules before optimization. For example, a job requiring a helper should only appear in route candidates whose crew pattern includes sufficient staffing.

\subsection*{4.3 Candidate route as a generated object}

Each candidate route $r \in R$ is a tuple

\[
r = (J_r, S_r, d(r), t(r), c(r), v(r)),
\]

where:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $J_r \subseteq J$ is the set of jobs included in route candidate $r$;
    \item $S_r = \{s(j) : j \in J_r\}$ is the set of associated stop groups;
    \item $d(r) \in D$ is the assigned planning day of the candidate;
    \item $t(r)$ is the route type label;
    \item $c(r)$ is the set of feasible crew patterns for the candidate;
    \item $v(r)$ is the set of feasible vehicles for the candidate.
\end{itemize}

The route candidate set $R$ is therefore not arbitrary. It is the output of a rule-based generation map

\[
\Gamma : (J,S,A,\text{policy},\text{exceptions}) \mapsto R.
\]

\subsection*{4.4 Feasibility conditions for candidate generation}

A subset of jobs $J_r$ may be turned into a candidate route only if the following conditions hold during preprocessing:

\begin{align*}
& e_j = 1, && \forall j \in J_r, \\
& \eta_{s(j)s(j')} = 1 \text{ or } d_{s(j)s(j')} \le \bar{d}_{\text{geo}}, && \forall j,j' \in J_r, \\
& \phi_{j,t(r)} = 1, && \forall j \in J_r, \\
& \exists c \in C,\; v \in V \text{ such that the route type and staffing rules are satisfied.}
\end{align*}

Here $\bar{d}_{\text{geo}}$ is a configurable geographic coherence threshold. It controls how far apart stops may be before they should no longer belong to the same candidate route.

\subsection*{4.5 Geography score construction}

The geography quality score $g_r$ used later in the optimization may be computed from the route candidate geometry. One generic form is

\[
g_r = w_1 \cdot \operatorname{compact}(S_r) + w_2 \cdot \operatorname{adjacent}(S_r) - w_3 \cdot \operatorname{spread}(S_r) - w_4 \cdot \operatorname{cross}(S_r),
\]

where:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $\operatorname{compact}(S_r)$ rewards dense and localized stop patterns;
    \item $\operatorname{adjacent}(S_r)$ rewards movement through neighboring stop groups;
    \item $\operatorname{spread}(S_r)$ penalizes large geographic dispersion;
    \item $\operatorname{cross}(S_r)$ penalizes unnecessary cross-area mixing.
\end{itemize}

The term $\operatorname{cross}(S_r)$ should be treated as a soft penalty, not a hard prohibition, because the brief states that area boundaries are guidance rather than hard walls.

\subsection*{4.6 Relationship to the optimization model}

After preprocessing, the generated route set $R$ is treated as fixed input to the optimization problem. The tensors in the optimization model are induced by the candidate routes:

\[
a_{jr} = \mathbf{1}\{j \in J_r\}, \qquad
\delta_{rd} = \mathbf{1}\{d(r)=d\}, \qquad
f_{rcv} = \mathbf{1}\{c \in c(r),\; v \in v(r)\}.
\]

This separation is important. Geography and business rules are used first to construct a tractable set of meaningful route candidates. The optimization model then selects the best weekly combination among those candidates.

\section*{5. Sets and Indices}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\toprule
Symbol & Meaning \\
\midrule
$J$ & set of candidate jobs for the week \\
$J^{sp} \subseteq J$ & set of jobs requiring special-route handling \\
$R$ & set of feasible candidate route-day proposals \\
$R^{sp} \subseteq R$ & set of special-route candidates \\
$R^{reg} = R \setminus R^{sp}$ & set of regular route candidates \\
$C$ & set of feasible crew patterns \\
$V$ & set of vehicles \\
$D$ & set of planning days in the week \\
$A$ & set of business areas \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The set $C$ may contain both one-technician crews and helper-required crew patterns. This lets the model represent two-man work without introducing stop-level sequencing.

\section*{6. Parameters}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\toprule
Symbol & Meaning \\
\midrule
$a_{jr} \in \{0,1\}$ & equals $1$ if job $j$ is included in route candidate $r$ \\
$\delta_{rd} \in \{0,1\}$ & equals $1$ if route candidate $r$ is planned for day $d$ \\
$f_{rcv} \in \{0,1\}$ & equals $1$ if route $r$ is feasible with crew $c$ and vehicle $v$ \\
$p_j \ge 0$ & base business priority of job $j$ \\
$u_j \ge 0$ & standby usefulness score of job $j$ \\
$g_r \ge 0$ & geography quality score of route $r$ \\
$h_j \ge 0$ & fairness or aging pressure for job $j$ \\
$\ell_{ar} \ge 0$ & readiness contribution of route $r$ to area $a$ \\
$H_{cd} \in \mathbb{Z}_{+}$ & available route slots for crew $c$ on day $d$ \\
$Q_{vd} \in \mathbb{Z}_{+}$ & available route slots for vehicle $v$ on day $d$ \\
$b_j \in \{0,1\}$ & equals $1$ if job $j$ is eligible for standby output \\
$\kappa_j \ge 0$ & special-route priority weight for job $j \in J^{sp}$ \\
$\alpha_s, \beta_s, \gamma_s, \mu_s \ge 0$ & seasonal objective weights for season $s$ \\
$\theta_a^M, \theta_a^G \ge 0$ & readiness thresholds for area $a$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The seasonal weights allow the planner to shift emphasis across the year:

\begin{itemize}
    \item in the warm season, geography receives more weight;
    \item in the cold season, fairness and aging receive more weight.
\end{itemize}

\section*{7. Decision Variables}

\begin{align*}
x_{rcv} &\in \{0,1\} && \text{equals $1$ if route candidate $r$ is selected with crew $c$ and vehicle $v$} \\
y_j &\in \{0,1\} && \text{equals $1$ if job $j$ is scheduled this week} \\
z_j &\in \{0,1\} && \text{equals $1$ if job $j$ is placed in the standby pool} \\
\rho_a &\ge 0 && \text{readiness score for area $a$}
\end{align*}

\section*{8. Core Feasibility Constraints}

\subsection*{8.1 Route assignment feasibility}

Only crew-vehicle combinations declared feasible by preprocessing may be selected:

\[
x_{rcv} \le f_{rcv}, \qquad \forall r \in R,\; c \in C,\; v \in V.
\]

\subsection*{8.2 Job uniqueness}

Each job may appear in at most one selected route:

\[
\sum_{r \in R} \sum_{c \in C} \sum_{v \in V} a_{jr} x_{rcv} \le 1, \qquad \forall j \in J.
\]

We link the route decisions to the weekly scheduled-job variable:

\[
y_j = \sum_{r \in R} \sum_{c \in C} \sum_{v \in V} a_{jr} x_{rcv}, \qquad \forall j \in J.
\]

\subsection*{8.3 Crew capacity by day}

Each crew pattern has limited weekly availability, after weekly exceptions have been applied:

\[
\sum_{r \in R} \sum_{v \in V} \delta_{rd} x_{rcv} \le H_{cd}, \qquad \forall c \in C,\; d \in D.
\]

\subsection*{8.4 Vehicle capacity by day}

Each vehicle has limited availability by day:

\[
\sum_{r \in R} \sum_{c \in C} \delta_{rd} x_{rcv} \le Q_{vd}, \qquad \forall v \in V,\; d \in D.
\]

\subsection*{8.5 Standby eligibility}

A standby job cannot also be scheduled:

\[
z_j \le 1 - y_j, \qquad \forall j \in J.
\]

Only jobs marked as standby-eligible may appear in the standby pool:

\[
z_j \le b_j, \qquad \forall j \in J.
\]

\section*{9. Lexicographic Optimization Structure}

The brief says that special route types should be handled before normal route filling. A natural mathematical representation is a \emph{lexicographic} or \emph{two-stage} optimization.

\subsection*{Stage I: prioritize special-route work}

First solve:

\[
\max \; Z_1 = \sum_{j \in J^{sp}} \kappa_j y_j
\]

subject to the core feasibility constraints above.

Let $Z_1^{\star}$ denote the optimal value.

\subsection*{Stage II: optimize the weekly draft plan}

Then solve the secondary problem:

\[
\max \; Z_2 =
\alpha_s \sum_{j \in J} p_j y_j
+ \beta_s \sum_{r \in R} \sum_{c \in C} \sum_{v \in V} g_r x_{rcv}
+ \gamma_s \sum_{j \in J} h_j y_j
+ \mu_s \sum_{j \in J} u_j z_j
\]

subject to:

\begin{align*}
& \sum_{j \in J^{sp}} \kappa_j y_j = Z_1^{\star}, \\
& \text{all core feasibility constraints.}
\end{align*}

This means that special-route work is optimized first, and then the normal weekly tradeoff between job value, geographic quality, fairness pressure, and standby quality is optimized without sacrificing the best achievable special-route coverage.

\section*{10. Interpretation of the Objective}

The secondary objective has four terms:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item $\sum_{j \in J} p_j y_j$: rewards scheduling high-value jobs.
    \item $\sum_{r,c,v} g_r x_{rcv}$: rewards geographically coherent routes.
    \item $\sum_{j \in J} h_j y_j$: rewards fairness and aging pressure.
    \item $\sum_{j \in J} u_j z_j$: rewards standby jobs that are useful substitutes if a scheduled job drops out.
\end{enumerate}

The seasonal coefficient vector $(\alpha_s, \beta_s, \gamma_s, \mu_s)$ changes by season. For example:

\begin{itemize}
    \item in March through September, choose $\beta_s$ relatively large so geography dominates;
    \item in October through February, increase $\gamma_s$ so fairness and aging carry more influence.
\end{itemize}

\section*{11. Area Readiness Output}

Area Readiness is not treated as a hard constraint in Version 1. Instead, it is computed from the selected routes:

\[
\rho_a = \sum_{r \in R} \sum_{c \in C} \sum_{v \in V} \ell_{ar} x_{rcv}, \qquad \forall a \in A.
\]

The plain-language readiness label for each area is then derived post-optimization:

\[
\text{Readiness}(a) =
\begin{cases}
\text{Good}, & \rho_a \ge \theta_a^G, \\
\text{Moderate}, & \theta_a^M \le \rho_a < \theta_a^G, \\
\text{Lean}, & \rho_a < \theta_a^M.
\end{cases}
\]

This is consistent with the brief: Area Readiness remains a communication layer that should stay credible relative to actual run behavior.

\section*{12. Notes on Preprocessing}

Several business requirements are most naturally enforced before optimization:

\begin{itemize}
    \item candidate pool eligibility;
    \item duplicate-address grouping;
    \item route-day candidate generation;
    \item route-type tagging;
    \item crew and vehicle feasibility screening.
\end{itemize}

Mathematically, those steps determine the data tensors $a_{jr}$, $\delta_{rd}$, $f_{rcv}$, and $\ell_{ar}$.

In particular, the statement ``area boundaries are guidance, not hard walls'' is implemented by allowing cross-area candidate routes during preprocessing whenever their geography score remains strong.

\section*{13. Why This Formulation Fits Version 1}

This formulation is appropriate for Version 1 because it is:

\begin{itemize}
    \item route-first rather than appointment-first;
    \item explainable to a non-technical owner;
    \item consistent with human review;
    \item compatible with Airtable-managed policy inputs;
    \item lighter than exact tour optimization;
    \item able to encode special-route precedence and seasonal fairness tradeoffs.
\end{itemize}

The model is also a practical bridge to implementation in Pyomo. The binary variables $x_{rcv}$, $y_j$, and $z_j$ lead directly to a mixed-integer linear programming model once the candidate route set has been generated.

\section*{14. Explicitly Out of Scope}

The following elements are intentionally excluded from the Version 1 optimization model:

\begin{itemize}
    \item exact stop sequencing within a route;
    \item appointment-time assignment;
    \item direct ServiceM8 calendar writes;
    \item speculative machine learning components;
    \item fully global stop-level vehicle routing across the whole week.
\end{itemize}
